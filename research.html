Researching how autonomous systems should behave.

Symbia Labs studies identity, continuity, and governance for AI systems that operate over time, make decisions, and must be held accountable.

Focus

Execution-first systems work, not speculative hype.

Promise

Behavior that can be governed and examined.

Scope

Above models and protocols; at the execution layer.

Method

Architectures, proofs, and reference implementations.

Framing

Research agenda

Most AI research focuses on models: making them larger, faster, or more capable. Our research asks a different question: what surrounds the model when it is deployed into real institutions, workflows, and responsibilities?

Themes

Core research themes

Execution layers for AI

We study how to structure systems above models and protocols that provide identity, state, constraints, and traceability.

Identity and continuity

We explore what it means for a system to have a durable identity across time, deployments, and interactions—without anthropomorphism.

Epistemic contracts

We research how systems should represent knowledge, inference, and uncertainty explicitly, rather than hiding them inside prompts or logs.

Governance by construction

We investigate architectures where policy and constraint enforcement are structural properties of the runtime, not after-the-fact controls.

Positioning

Protocol is not cognition

We treat MCP, HTTP, MQTT, and similar technologies as transport and interface layers. Our research focuses on the execution layer above them—where meaning, responsibility, and governance actually live.

Outputs

Research outputs

Position papers and whitepapers.

Open specifications (e.g. identity and epistemic contracts).

Reference implementations used to test architectural claims.

Relationship

Research vs. product

Symbia Labs conducts research and publishes open specifications. Symbia Seed is a governed runtime that embodies these ideas. The research informs the product, but the product does not define the research.

Closing

This is long-horizon work.

Our goal is not to chase benchmarks or demos. It is to define the boundary conditions required for AI systems to function as accountable participants in human institutions.
